What Is Thermography ROI and How Is It Calculated for a Clinic?

Thermography ROI is the ratio of net revenue generated by infrared imaging studies to the total cost of equipment, software, training, and ongoing fees.

ROI = (annual scan revenue − annual costs) ÷ total investment, expressed as months-to-payback.

According to Med Hot, a realistic calculation includes the camera, calibration, DICOM (Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine — the standard format for medical image storage) integration, staff time, and software licensing. A clinic charging $295 per scan and performing 10 scans per week generates roughly $153,000 in annual gross revenue. Subtracting $18,000 in equipment amortization, $2,400 in software, and $12,000 in chair time leaves a net of approximately $120,000. Against a $28,000 system, that produces a payback period under 4 months at full volume — though most practices ramp slowly and realize true ROI between months 9 and 14.

Why Do CPT Codes Matter Less Than Cash-Pay Pricing for Thermography ROI?

CPT codes for thermography are the billing identifiers (primarily 93740 for temperature gradient studies and Category III codes for infrared imaging) that determine whether insurers reimburse.

Most major insurers classify thermography as investigational and do not reimburse, making cash-pay the dominant revenue model.

According to the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, thermography is listed as a non-covered service under most National Coverage Determinations (source: cms.gov). That sounds like bad news but actually simplifies ROI math — clinics collect at the time of service, eliminating denial cycles, accounts-receivable lag, and clawbacks. Med Hot's pricing guidance reflects this: cash-pay studies between $200 and $450 deliver predictable revenue per scan with zero billing overhead.

When Do Most Clinics Reach Breakeven on Their Thermography Investment?

Breakeven is the point at which cumulative scan revenue equals total investment in equipment, software, and onboarding.

Most U.S. clinics reach thermography breakeven between months 6 and 14, depending on patient volume and pricing.

Experts at Med Hot recommend modeling three scenarios — conservative, expected, and aggressive — before financing. Even the conservative scenario typically clears breakeven within 18 months on a $24,000 system. The single biggest accelerator is internal marketing: practices that introduce thermography to existing patients during the first 90 days reach breakeven 4 to 6 months sooner than practices that wait for outside referrals.

What Credentials Should Clinics Verify Before Buying Thermography Equipment?

Verify FDA 510(k) clearance, HIPAA-compliant software, and a signed Business Associate Agreement.

Thermography cameras sold for clinical use must hold an FDA 510(k) clearance for the intended use (verify via the FDA 510(k) database at accessdata.fda.gov). Software handling protected health information must comply with the HIPAA Security Rule (source: hhs.gov). Practitioners should also confirm their state scope-of-practice rules through their state licensing board — for example, the American Chiropractic Association maintains current state-by-state guidance (source: acatoday.org). Interpreting physicians or thermographers may pursue board certification through bodies such as the American College of Clinical Thermology.
